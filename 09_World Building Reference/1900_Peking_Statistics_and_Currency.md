# Peking Statistics and Currency (circa 1900)

Understanding the demographics, economics, and logistics of Peking in 1900 is crucial for grounding the story and determining how Kindred (vampires) would survive, hunt, and exert influence.

## 1. Population Demographics
* **Total Empire Population:** Approximately 400 to 450 million.
* **Peking Population:** Estimated between **700,000 and 1,000,000** people.
  * *Kindred Impact:* A population of 1 million can comfortably support a massive Kindred population (traditionally 1 vampire per 100,000 mortals is the Camarilla safe ratio, but in ancient strongholds like Peking, there could easily be 50+ Kindred operating without significantly depleting the blood supply).
* **The Foreign Presence (Pre-Siege):** 
  * Diplomats, foreign civilians, and missionaries: roughly 400 to 500.
  * Legation Guards: roughly 400.
  * This tiny foreign population of <1,000 individuals lived in a bubble surrounded by nearly a million resentful locals.
* **Chinese Christians:** Approximately 3,000 to 4,000 in the capital (who sought refuge in the Legations and the Beitang Cathedral).

## 2. Currency and Wealth
The Qing monetary system was notoriously complicated, relying on a bimetallic standard of unminted silver for large transactions and copper coins for daily life.

* **The Tael (Liang):** The Tael was not a coin, but a unit of weight in silver (approximately 37 grams). Silver ingots (called sycees) had to be weighed and assayed by moneychangers for every major transaction. 
  * *Value:* 1 Tael was a significant sum. The indemnity of the Boxer Protocol was 450 million Taels.
* **Copper Cash (Wen):** The everyday currency of the common peasant. These were cast bronze/copper coins with a square hole in the center, usually strung together on strings of 1,000.
  * *Exchange Rate:* Theoretically, 1 Tael of silver = 1,000 Copper Cash. In reality, inflation pushed this to 1 Tael = 1,200 to 1,600 Cash.
* **Silver Dollars (Yuan):** Because weighing silver was tedious, foreign silver coins (especially the Mexican Silver Dollar) circulated heavily. By the 1890s, the Qing government began minting its own "Dragon Dollars." 1 Silver Dollar was generally worth about 0.72 Taels.
* **Wages:** 
  * A common laborer or rickshaw puller might earn 15 to 30 copper cash a day. 
  * A standard Tael of silver could represent 1 to 2 months' wages for a peasant. 
  * *Kindred Impact:* Wealth disparity was astronomical. A European Ventrue or Manchu Elder carrying a few silver ingots possesses enough wealth to buy the absolute loyalty of a small mortal army or bribe any local magistrate.

## 3. Logistics and City Life (Storytelling Hooks)
* **The City Gates:** Peking was a heavily walled fortress city. The massive city gates (separating the Tartar City, the Chinese City, and the exterior) were **locked every night at sundown and did not open until dawn.** 
  * *Kindred Impact:* This is the most critical geographical feature for a vampire. If a Kindred is hunting in the Chinese City and fails to return to the Tartar City before the gates close, they are locked out for the entire night. It strictly divides hunting grounds and territories.
* **Transportation:** There were no paved roads suitable for cars. Travel within the city was done via palanquin (sedan chair carried by servants), mule cart, or horseback. Rickshaws, imported from Japan, were just becoming popular.
  * *Kindred Impact:* Traveling discreetly requires mortal retainers to carry you in a palanquin. The streets were notoriously dusty in summer and deep with mud in the rain.
* **Communication:** The telegraph existed but was entirely controlled by the government and foreign legations (until the Boxers cut the lines). The Imperial Post was slow. Most local, secure communication (especially among secret societies or Kindred) relied entirely on physical couriers and runners navigating the labyrinthine *hutongs* (alleys).
