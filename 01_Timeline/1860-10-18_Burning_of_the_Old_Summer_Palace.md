# The Burning of the Old Summer Palace (October 1860)

Forty years before the Boxer Rebellion, the Qing Court suffered a catastrophic trauma during the Second Opium War (1856–1860) that permanently scarred their relationship with the West and heavily influenced Empress Dowager Cixi's later paranoia.

## The Atrocity and Retaliation
* In September 1860, as Anglo-French forces marched on Peking, Qing forces captured a British and French diplomatic envoy under a flag of truce. The diplomats and their Sikh escorts were imprisoned, horrifically tortured, and many were murdered.
* In retaliation for the torture of diplomatic envoys, the British High Commissioner, Lord Elgin, ordered the complete destruction of the *Yuanmingyuan* (the Old Summer Palace)—the Emperor's magnificent, sprawling complex of palaces, gardens, and vast art collections located just northwest of Peking.

## The Inferno
* On October 18, 1860, over 3,000 British troops set the massive complex ablaze. The fire burned for three days, creating a massive cloud of black smoke that hung over Peking.
* The French and British troops engaged in an absolute frenzy of looting, stealing countless priceless artifacts, jades, porcelains, and the imperial Pekinese dogs.

## Storytelling Hook
* **The Scar on Cixi:** Empress Dowager Cixi (then a young concubine) was forced to flee the capital in terror as the palace burned. The profound humiliation of seeing the crown jewel of the empire reduced to ashes by "barbarians" instilled in her an eternal, burning hatred for foreigners. When the Eight-Nation Alliance marched on Peking again in 1900, she saw history repeating itself.
* **Kindred Impact:** The Old Summer Palace was a sprawling, ancient domain. Its destruction was an apocalypse for any Elder vampires who held court in its gardens or hoarded occult lore in its libraries. The trauma of 1860 is why the Kindred of Peking deeply fear and despise foreign intervention.
