# The Succession Crisis of 1875 (January 13, 1875)

Empress Dowager Cixi's grip on power was severely threatened when her biological son, the Tongzhi Emperor, suddenly died at the age of 18 without leaving an heir.

## The Death of Tongzhi
* The official palace records state the Tongzhi Emperor died of smallpox, though widespread, persistent rumors claimed he actually died of syphilis contracted during incognito visits to Peking's brothels.
* His death created an unprecedented crisis: Cixi's status as Empress Dowager and co-regent was entirely dependent on being the mother of the reigning Emperor. With him dead, she was supposed to retire.

## The Usurpation of Tradition
* Desperate to maintain her power, Cixi engineered a brazen violation of sacred dynastic succession laws. 
* By tradition, a new emperor had to be chosen from the next generation down so he could properly perform ancestral rites for the deceased emperor. 
* Instead, Cixi forced the selection of her own three-year-old nephew, Zaitian (the son of her sister and Prince Chun), who was of the *same* generation as the dead emperor.

## The Boy Emperor Guangxu
* She formally adopted the toddler as her own son and named him the Guangxu Emperor. Because he was an infant, Cixi retained her absolute power as regent for another two decades.
* **Storytelling Hook:** This ruthless manipulation of the succession laws deeply alienated conservative Manchu scholars and fueled secret palace intrigue. It also perfectly sets the stage for 1898—Guangxu was practically a prisoner to Cixi his entire life, explaining his desperate, radical attempt to break free during the Hundred Days' Reform.
