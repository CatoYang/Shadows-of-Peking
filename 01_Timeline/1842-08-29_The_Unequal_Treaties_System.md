# The Unequal Treaties and Extraterritoriality (1842 onwards)

While the Treaty of Shimonoseki in 1895 was a massive shock, it was actually part of a long chain of "Unequal Treaties" that began decades earlier, setting the stage for the intense anti-foreign hatred of the Boxer Rebellion.

## The Century of Humiliation
* The system began in 1842 with the **Treaty of Nanjing**, ending the First Opium War with Great Britain. 
* Over the next 50 years, after losing subsequent wars to Britain, France, and others, the Qing Dynasty was repeatedly forced to sign treaties that severely compromised Chinese sovereignty.

## Key Features of the Unequal Treaties
* **Extraterritoriality:** The most hated aspect of the treaties. It meant that foreign citizens in China were completely immune to Chinese laws. If a foreigner committed a crime, they could only be tried by their own consul. This essentially made foreigners untouchable by local magistrates.
* **Treaty Ports:** China was forced to open dozens of coastal and inland port cities (like Shanghai and Tianjin) to foreign trade and residence. Foreigners established "Concessions" in these cities—neighborhoods entirely governed and policed by foreign powers, where Chinese laws did not apply.
* **Missionary Protections:** The treaties explicitly guaranteed the right of foreign missionaries to travel anywhere in China, buy land, build churches, and proselytize. Because missionaries enjoyed extraterritoriality, they often used it to shield their Chinese converts from local justice, deeply disrupting village life.
* **Tariff Control:** The treaties fixed Chinese import tariffs at an incredibly low 5%, preventing China from protecting its own domestic industries and ensuring the economy was dominated by foreign goods.
