# Emperor Guangxu Assumes Personal Rule (February 1889)

After ruling "from behind the curtains" for nearly three decades, Empress Dowager Cixi formally handed the reins of government over to the 18-year-old Emperor Guangxu, though the transfer of power was largely an illusion.

## The Formal Transition
* In February 1889, following his grand imperial wedding (which Cixi meticulously arranged to ensure the new Empress was her own niece), Emperor Guangxu officially assumed *qinzheng* (personal rule).
* Empress Dowager Cixi announced her "retirement" from politics and moved to the newly rebuilt Summer Palace (*Yiheyuan*) on the outskirts of Peking. 
* **The Naval Controversy:** The rebuilding of this magnificent pleasure palace was deeply controversial, as Cixi funded the massive construction project by secretly diverting millions of taels of silver away from the modernization of the Beiyang Fleet (the Imperial Navy)—a decision that would prove catastrophic during the Sino-Japanese War five years later.

## The Illusion of Independence
* Although Guangxu now sat on the throne and conducted daily court audiences without a silk screen hiding his aunt, his independence was a sham.
* Cixi required the young Emperor to read all important memorials (petitions) to her and seek her approval before making any major policy decisions. Furthermore, the court was completely packed with her conservative loyalists who reported Guangxu's every move back to the Summer Palace.
* **Storytelling Hook:** This creates a brilliant, tense political dynamic in the capital. There are effectively two courts: the formal court of the Emperor in the Forbidden City, and the true seat of power with the retired Empress Dowager at the Summer Palace. Kindred trying to influence mortal politics must carefully navigate between these two intensely paranoid, rival courts.
