# The "Er Maozi" and Social Friction (1890s)

A critical factor in the ramp-up to the Boxer Rebellion was the intense, everyday social friction between traditional Chinese villagers and Chinese Christian converts, whom the Boxers derogatorily called *Er Maozi* ("Secondary Devils" or "Second-Class Hairy Men").

## Missionary Lawsuits
* Under the unequal treaties, foreign missionaries enjoyed extraterritoriality and absolute protection. 
* Missionaries frequently intervened in local Chinese lawsuits on behalf of their Chinese converts. Fearful of angering foreign powers, local Qing magistrates almost always ruled in favor of the Christians, regardless of the facts of the case.
* Criminals sometimes "converted" to Christianity solely to escape justice or steal land from their neighbors with foreign backing.

## Cultural Disruption
* Traditional village life was funded by communal taxes used for local temple festivals, opera performances, and rainmaking rituals. 
* Chinese Christians, instructed by missionaries that these were "pagan" rituals, refused to pay these communal taxes. 
* When the droughts of 1898-1899 hit, non-Christians furiously blamed the converts. They believed the Christians' refusal to participate in rituals and the presence of their foreign churches (which disrupted Feng Shui) had deeply angered the heavens and caused the famine.
